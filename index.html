The turn

02 / WHY

For years, the ticket would land and I'd build it. Cleanly, on time, well-tested. Then I'd ship it and watch what happened next. That's where it got interesting.

Sometimes the thing I built was exactly what people needed. Sometimes it was technically perfect and quietly ignored. I started noticing the gap. The difference between how well something is built and whether it should have been built at all.

That gap is the whole job of product. The decision sits one layer above the code. In the conversations with users. In the trade-offs between three stakeholders who all want different things. In the discipline of saying no to good ideas so a great one has room to breathe.

"How do I build it?" is a craft question. "Why are we building it, for whom, and what happens if we don't?" That's a different muscle entirely. I want to train it.

The engineer in me isn't going anywhere. But the next version of my work lives one step earlier in the cycle. Closer to the user. Closer to the question. Closer to the room where the why gets decided.

What it actually means

03 / THE WORK

I used to think product was just "deciding what to build." It's so much more than that. The real work is in the middle. Between the user, the engineers, the designers, the leadership, and the constraints nobody talks about.

01 · DISCOVERY
Listening before deciding

Talking to users, watching how they actually behave, separating what people say they want from what they'll actually use. The hardest part is being willing to be wrong.

02 · STAKEHOLDERS
Aligning people who disagree

Engineering wants time. Sales wants the deal. Leadership wants the metric. The user wants something simple. A PM holds all of them at once and finds a path everyone can live with.

03 · PRIORITIZATION
Saying no, a lot

The roadmap isn't a wish list. It's a series of bets, each one made by quietly killing five other good ideas. Learning to do this cleanly, without losing people, is half the craft.

04 · COMMUNICATION
Writing as the main artifact

PRDs, one-pagers, async updates, the Slack message before the meeting. The clearest thinker in the room often wins by writing it down first, plainly, before anyone else can muddy it.

Working through the craft

05 / MILESTONES

Inside the PM fellowship I'm working through structured milestones. Each one is a chance to think like a PM about a real product. My current case study: why ChatGPT's voice feature isn't being adopted in India.

M01
Systems Thinking & Mapping Outcomes
Understanding the landscape · mapping business and product outcomes

Voice adoption in India is a context problem, not a tech problem. 700M+ mobile-first users, real typing friction, vernacular usage. Voice should dominate. But it only wins where it's faster than typing. YouTube search. WhatsApp voice notes. Google Assistant. ChatGPT's voice sits in none of those slots.

Where voice wins
Speed, multitasking, hands-free context
Where it loses
Privacy concerns · ambient noise · habit inertia · accuracy with Hinglish
ChatGPT's gap
Voice exists but lacks discoverability, clear use case, and contextual trigger
The voice usage equation I built
Voice Usage = Total Users × % Using Voice × Frequency per User
% Using Voice ← discoverability, trust, ease of use
Frequency ← use-case relevance, speed vs. typing, habit formation
Voice adoption won't grow by improving the feature alone. It will grow when voice becomes the default, fastest, and most intuitive way to interact.
M02
User Research & Problem Framing
Segmenting users · running research · deriving insights

I segmented ChatGPT users by intent and went deep on learning-focused students in Tier 2 & 3 cities. Largest growing segment, daily usage, and they live in the exact shared-space environments where the voice-vs-typing call gets made.

Hypothesis

Students in Tier 2/3 cities aren't using voice because it feels uncomfortable in shared environments, they don't trust its accuracy with Hinglish and accents, and typing still feels more reliable and controllable.

Quantitative · Survey
  • 60–70% of users have never used voice input
  • More than half feel uncomfortable speaking in public spaces
  • Nearly half prefer typing because it feels more reliable
  • A significant share called out voice accuracy issues
Qualitative · Interviews
  • "I mostly use ChatGPT in hostel or class. Speaking out loud just feels awkward."
  • "Voice didn't understand my words properly, so I had to repeat."
  • "Typing feels safer. I can edit before sending."
  • "I've never really thought about using voice. Typing is just default."
Core insight

Users aren't avoiding voice because they don't know about it. They avoid it because it feels uncomfortable in their environment, not fully reliable, and not clearly better than typing in their current setup.

Prioritization · RISE framework
ReachHighLarge student base
ImpactHighFaster input → more usage
ConfidenceMediumBased on early research
EffortMediumUX + trust changes

Problems I actually solved

07 / CASE STUDIES

Four problems, four products, four shipped solutions. Each one taught me something I couldn't have learned by reading.

CASE 01 B2C · VOICE AI
Libero: making voice feel present, not pushy
Problem
Voice assistants either talked too much or cut off too fast. People dropped them inside a week.
What I did
Tied response length to query length. Short questions got short answers. The follow-up became the user's job, not the system's.
Outcome
Repeat sessions in my test group went from 2.3 to 5.6 per week. Users stopped saying "the AI" and started saying "Libero".
CASE 02 B2C · SOCIAL
Socio: cutting 14 of 17 features to find the product
Problem
My v0 had 17 features and felt like a knock-off of Instagram. Test users said exactly that.
What I did
Asked 8 users one question: keep only three things, what would they be? Six picked the same three. Everything else got cut.
Outcome
78% daily active in test group, week one. The "this is just Instagram" feedback disappeared. People started inviting friends unprompted.
VIEW REPO
CASE 03 E-COMMERCE · CONVERSION
Nuox: rebuilding checkout around a single job
Problem
Cart-to-purchase sat at 31%. Heatmaps showed users hesitating on a 9-field address form.
What I did
Reordered the form to PIN-first (auto-fills four fields), set UPI as the default payment, and pinned the order summary to a sticky bottom bar.
Outcome
Conversion: 31% to 47%. Time-to-purchase: 2m 40s to 1m 12s. The address-step drop-off was cut in half.
VIEW REPO
CASE 04 FITNESS · LANDING
Gym landing: turning a page into a self-qualifying funnel
Problem
Paid traffic bounced at 84%. The page was selling to bodybuilders. The actual audience was 80% women in their 30s.
What I did
Rebuilt around one promise ("a gym that doesn't make you feel watched"), added a 3-question fit quiz, swapped testimonials to match the real segment.
Outcome
Bounce: 84% to 51%. Trial form completions tripled from 11 a week to 38. Half of new walk-ins now cite the quiz.
